Teaching philosophy

​Traditional lecture-based teaching with little student participation has long been known to ineffectively foster undergraduate learning. Reflecting on my own experiences as an undergrad, I "learned by doing" via laboratory tasks, presentations, and writing assignments, rather than memorizing and regurgitating information. Some of my most effective instructors combined different types of activities (class brain-storming, group break-out sessions, debates, games) to gain a deeper understanding of the material. Such “active learning” has been demonstrated to increase student test scores, and student perceptions of their own learning. The challenge is finding a way to spark curiosity in students, regardless of the specific subject being covered. If you are interested in learning more about my teaching philosophy, you can access my full teaching statement here.

What is your favorite microbe?

I had the privilege of volunteering at IGB’s largest science outreach event, the World of Genomics (WOG), held last year (2019) at the National Academy of Sciences (NAS) in Washington, D.C. We wanted to use the event to introduce children and their parents to the importance of mobile DNA - bits of DNA that can be picked up from the environment or another organism to encode a novel function in the bearer. Unlike DNA that is inherited, passed down from one generation to the next, mobile DNA can be acquired even within the same generation. Next Gen Sequencing has shown that mobile DNA elements are widespread, found in organisms from every domain of life. But they are especially common in microbes. ​

But how would you explain these concepts to young kids who have no background in genomics or biology and probably just want to play? ​Most of my past experience with SciComm has involved explaining my science to other researchers and those familiar with my study system, and I did not know much about designing card games for children. But I had tremendous help from the IGB Science Outreach Team headed by Dr. Courtney Fenlon as well as IGOH’s theme leader Dr. Rachel Whitaker and many of the theme members, especially my own supervisor Dr. Katy Heath. You can read more about the process of how I went about designing the game in IGB's Zine here. But in the end, I realized that the key part of the design process is one that envelopes all the steps. That’s to think all the time about why you love a field of study and why you want to share it. I have presented the game at different venues since then, and every time learn a new way to get others excited about science. Suffice to say, it's been such a rewarding experience!
