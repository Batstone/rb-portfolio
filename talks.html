Giving effective scientific presentations

I think I am in the minority to say that I actually enjoy giving talks. Do I get nervous beforehand? Yes! My palms sweat like crazy and my heart starts racing. But I always end up finding the process of putting a talk together, and then presenting in front of colleagues, a super rewarding experience. The art of not just presenting research, but communicating it in a way an audience can follow, is super challenging, but for me, is worth investing in and improving.

Recently, I gave a guest lecture for the undergrad course "Training in Team Science" (IB299C) at the IGB on how to give an effective scientific presentation. To prepare, I made a list of all the tips and tricks I felt were important to keep in mind, using advice I've been given by past and current supervisors, fellow postdocs and grad students, and even my own family (who often are subject to me practicing talks in front of them). In the process, I ended up compiling a cheat-sheet of sorts (click image for link), which I hoped students would find useful when they go to make their own presentations, but could be a good resource for anyone.

Evolution 2015 - Brazil

This was my first oral presentation of my PhD, and I had only recently analyzed the data. It's interesting to see glimpses of the scientific journey, from designing an experiment, to collecting the data, interpreting what it means, and then eventually publishing the story. The time and mental energy spent to achieve the latter is easy to forget years later.
