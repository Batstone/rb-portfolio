Darwin's hidden bank - the microbiome

"It is interesting to contemplate a tangled bank, clothed with many plants of many kinds, with birds singing on the bushes, with various insects flitting about, and with worms crawling through the damp earth, and to reflect that these elaborately constructed forms, so different from each other, and dependent upon each other in so complex a manner, have all been produced by laws acting around us.” - Darwin, 1859

Kateryana Kon/Science Photo Library, via Getty Images

Darwin's tangled bank paints a picture of a "macrobial" world - one that's predominantly inhabited by plants, humans, and other animals. Since the dawn of the Next-Generation Sequencing era, however, it’s never been more clear that "macrobes" are actually newcomers in a microbial world, having (co)evolved with microbes since the beginning of their comparatively short evolutionary history. Many microbes, including bacteria, archaea, fungi, and viruses, are symbiotic, living on or within macrobial host tissues. Importantly, symbiotic microbes have the capacity to generate novel extended phenotypes that play vital roles in host fitness by affecting nutrient availability and uptake, resistance to pathogens and other host enemies, and even host behavior. Thus, symbiotic microbial genomes encode important, though often unknown, sources of genetic variation that potentially facilitate host adaptation. While Darwin and subsequent biologists have described the laws of evolution relevant to a macrobial world, advances in microbiology and molecular genetics call into question whether these laws need to be extended, or even rewritten, in a microbial and symbiotic world.

​My research program leverages classical evolutionary theory, population genomics, microbiology, experimental evolution, and bioinformatics to predict evolution when we explicitly account for symbiosis. Below I describe how my past work has used a variety of empirical approaches to address critical questions that drive my research program, then outline how I will expand this work in the future in my own lab.

Using the legume-rhizobium symbiosis as a focal interaction within a microbiome to better understand host-microbe coevolution

Many people depend on leguminous plants such as beans and peas as a major source of their daily protein. But what some might not know is that legumes are nitrogen rich because of their symbiotic relationship with bacteria. Bacteria, known as rhizobia, dwell in the soil for part of their life cycle, but it’s only when they intimately associate with a legume via symbiosis that they begin to fix nitrogen. The plant and its rhizobia partners enter in to a mutual exchange of resources – the plant provides carbon in the form of sugar to rhizobia. Reciprocally, rhizobia fix atmospheric nitrogen into usable forms for the plant, such as ammonia. The site of nutrient exchange takes place within nodules located on the legume’s roots, each nodule typically housing a single strain of rhizobia (Fig. 1A below).

This symbiosis contributes 70% of all biologically fixed N to the global N cycle, and thus, represents one of the most ecologically and economically valuable symbiotic interactions. It is also a relatively tractable system with many genomic and molecular resources available, making it an ideal model for understanding symbiosis evolution.

Fig. 1A: resource exchange between legumes and rhizobia

Illustration by Lu Loram Martin

Rhizobia genomes are especially interesting because they are multipartite, meaning they include a large chromosome as well as one or more symbiosis plasmids that can be considered distinct biological entities (Fig. 1B). Many of the genes involved in symbiotic functions, including nodulation and nitrogen fixation, are located on the plasmids.

Fig. 1B: Rhizobium genomes are multipartite: many of the genes involved in symbiosis are located on Mega-plasmids that can constitute almost 50% of the cell's entire genome.

Combining large-scale manipulative experiments with quantitative genetics to understand symbiosis evolution and stability

Empirical evidence robustly supports widespread fitness alignment within the legume-rhizobium symbiosis, meaning that more effective N-fixing rhizobia receive greater fitness rewards from their host plant relative to ineffective N-fixing rhizobia. Traits that allow a host to preferentially reward or associate with more beneficial symbionts (i.e., preference traits) are largely responsible for this alignment, yet we know little about how these traits evolve. Using an evolutionary quantitative genetics approach in the model legume Medicago truncatula and effective and ineffective strains of its Ensifer melilotisymbiont, I found that plants were under selection to favor the more effective N-fixer, rather than to punish or exclude the ineffective N-fixer (Batstone et al. 2017). Thus, the presence of effective mutualistic symbionts, rather than ineffective or ‘cheating’ symbionts, provide the selective incentive for host preference traits to evolve, ultimately maintaining symbiosis stability.​​

Experimental evolution coupled with bioinformatics uncovers the genomic underpinnings of microbial adaptation within symbiosis

Photo credit: Bill Cole

Photo credit: Megan Frederickson

In a recent publication in Science, we explored how beneficial microbes adapt to hosts. Advances in microbiome science require a better understanding of how beneficial microbes adapt to hosts. Mutualism theory predicts that choosy hosts should select for microbial partners that maximize host fitness (i.e., high-quality partners). As high-quality partners become fixed in a population, however, host preference paradoxically erodes the genetic variation in microbial quality required for host preference. Yet, variation in microbial quality is extensive in nature, begging the question: what factors maintain variation in partner quality? By combining a year-long evolution experiment, a cross-inoculation experiment, and whole genome resequencing, I overwhelmingly found that that variation in microbial quality was maintained by rapid adaptation of symbionts to their local host: isolates that shared an evolutionary history with a particular host genotype contributed more to that host's growth and achieved higher fitness than when paired with another host. Local adaptation resulted from genomic variants that had host-specific (i.e., conditionally-neutral) effects, rather than variants that were beneficial on one host but detrimental on another (i.e., antagonistic pleiotropy). Overall, these results suggest microbial adaptation is an important driver of genetic variation in microbial quality, given that adapting to one host genotype causes less favorable (but not necessarily deleterious) outcomes on another host genotype.

Symbiosis evolution under changing environmental conditions

Long term ecological research (LTER) plots located at the Kellogg Biological Station in Michigan. For the past 30 years, half of the experimental plots have been given additional N fertilizer, while the other half have not, allowing one to compare plant or soil communities between N-treatments

Experimentally evolving rhizobium under high and low-N conditions to recapitulate evolution occurring in the field. Pooled and whole-genome sequencing will be used to quantify relative strain fitness changes in allele frequencies, and prevalence of horizontal gene transfer in each community type and N-treatment.

Rhizobial adaptation to N-fertilization

Theory predicts that legumes should reduce symbiotic investment when abiotic N is abundant, given that symbiotically-derived N is more costly to acquire than N from the soil. Indeed, long-term N-fertilization was found to select for low-quality rhizobia (Weese et al. 2015), and the genetic basis for this shift occurred in mobile symbiosis plasmids (Klinger et al. 2016). However, the relative roles of standing genetic variation, novel mutations, and horizontal gene transfer (HGT) in rhizobial adaptation to high-N conditions remain unknown. I am currently conducting a large-scale evolution experiment that tests how N-fertilization affects rhizobial population composition and evolution over time. Specifically, I competed N-adapted and non-N-adapted (i.e., control) strains in single and mixed populations under high and low levels of N and across multiple plant populations (schematic above). By measuring strain frequencies in each population over time, I can test whether N-adapted strains outcompete control strains only under high-N, and whether N-adaptation is costly under N-limited conditions. I will also sequence the entire genomes of the strains at the end of the experiment to test how allele frequencies are impacted by N-level, and whether HGT occurred, given that many of the rhizobial genes involved in symbiosis are located on mobile plasmids or islands.

Future directions

Treating the legume-rhizobium symbiosis as a nested interaction within a complex phytobiome

The legume-rhizobium symbiosis has proven to be a powerful model for understanding how mutualistic microbial symbioses evolve and respond to changing environmental conditions. Using the toolkit I've built while working on this system, I will address the role of horizontal gene transfer (HGT) in natural rhizobia populations. It is currently unknown how frequently HGT occurs in nature, and what environmental cues might trigger it. I will test the role of HGT in invasion success, and specifically whether rhizobia can acquire plasmids or genes that help them spread across newly invaded sites. Rather than occurring in a vacuum, however, this symbiosis is embedded within a complex community of interacting microbes, the phytobiome. Moving forward, I will treat the legume-rhizobium symbiosis as a focal interaction within a phytobiome, ideally conducting similar experimental evolution approaches as described above, but within the context of naturally-occurring microbiomes. Overall, this work would help pinpoint the genetic mechanisms underlying microbial adaptation to stressful conditions, and what effects microbial evolution have on host fitness.

The legume-rhizobium symbiosis represents multiple nested layers of interaction. 1) Plasmids, or other mobile genetic elements, infect a rhizobium cell. 2) Once a rhizobium cell is infected by the ‘right’ plasmid, it can then infect a plant root. The interaction between a rhizobium chromosome and plasmid(s) is, thus, necessary to form the symbiosis with the legume, the second layer of the nested interaction. 3) Rhizobia divide and proliferate within nodules that eventually break open or senesce, releasing rhizobia into the soil. Here, rhizobia switch to a free-living lifestyle and are thought to exchange or acquire new plasmids, potentially giving them the ability to infect a different host. Understanding how these multiple distinct entities form nested interactions that produce the emergent traits we care about – for example, plant yield, is further complicated by the fact that they evolve within a tangled web of interactions involving millions of other microbes.
